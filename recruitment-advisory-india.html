The Root Problem

Most companies don't have a talent problem.
They have a decision problem.

The failure happens before the first CV is reviewed. The brief is misaligned, the evaluation criteria are wrong, or the wrong stakeholders are making the call. Leadership hiring fails because of process failure — not talent scarcity.

Where hiring decisions break down

Wrong brief. The JD describes the role as it existed — not as it needs to evolve. Every CV looks wrong because the spec is wrong.
Wrong evaluation signals. Brand-name employers, prestigious degrees, confident presentation — none of these predict performance in a specific context.
Wrong decision-makers in the room. Panels avoid conflict and default to the least objectionable option. Single managers optimise for people like themselves.
Wrong timeline. Urgency compresses process. Compressed process privileges available candidates over the right ones.
No post-hire accountability. The recruitment partner disappears at offer acceptance — the point where the real test begins.

Bangalore · Karnataka

Recruitment partner
in Bangalore.
Judgment over velocity.

Bangalore has more executive search firms and LinkedIn recruiters per capita than any other Indian city. What it lacks is decision discipline. The most common failure mode in leadership hiring in Bangalore is not a shortage of talent — it is availability mistaken for fit.

As a recruitment partner in Bangalore, we work with Series A–D founders and CXOs who understand the difference between a fast shortlist and the right decision.

Recurring hiring failure patterns in Bangalore

Brand-name bias. Hiring from Swiggy, Razorpay, or Flipkart does not guarantee the candidate can operate without the same structural support. The brand experience doesn't transfer automatically.
Urgency-driven shortlisting. Compressed timelines privilege candidates who are immediately available — systematically filtering out the strongest candidates, who are employed and not urgently looking.
Founders evaluating roles they haven't held. A technical founder interviewing a CFO — or a product founder assessing a CSO — is evaluating on instinct, not competency evidence.
The Series B trap. Hiring instincts that worked at 30 people produce the wrong leaders at 150. Stage-fit is a separate question from functional capability.
The Bangalore Pattern

Speed pressure forces premature shortlisting. Premature shortlisting produces underqualified hires. Three months later, the founder is back at the start — with a depleted timeline and a weaker position in the market.

Chennai · Tamil Nadu

Recruitment partner
in Chennai.
Execution depth. Leadership gap.

Chennai produces some of India's strongest operational talent. The city rewards diligence, institutional loyalty, and process adherence — genuine strengths that create a specific weakness at the leadership layer.

As a recruitment partner in Chennai, we do the alignment work before the search that determines whether an external hire can actually succeed inside an established, culture-strong organisation.

Where Chennai leadership hiring breaks down

Recruiting for transformation without internal readiness. Strong external hires fail when the organisation isn't prepared to receive change. The problem precedes the search.
Underestimating cultural capital. In Chennai organisations, institutional relationships and informal authority matter as much as formal title. External hires without that capital struggle even with strong mandates.
Candidate immobility. Senior professionals at ₹50L–₹1Cr CTC prefer to stay local. Metro-to-Chennai searches take longer than projected and often compromise on fit when this isn't accounted for upfront.
12–18 month exit cycle. Exits attributed to "culture fit" are usually the result of absent stakeholder alignment work before the search began.
Kerala

Recruitment partner
in Kerala.
Talent exists. Alignment doesn't.

Kerala has strong talent — across CA, legal, engineering, IT, and healthcare. The problem is that the most experienced professionals are either in metro cities or returning from Gulf markets with corporate playbooks calibrated for a fundamentally different operating environment.

As a recruitment partner in Kerala, we test for contextual adaptability — the capability most hiring processes don't assess and most failed hires lacked.

Kerala-specific hiring failure points

Gulf returnee misalignment. Senior professionals returning from UAE, Qatar, or Saudi Arabia carry titles and experience built in resource-rich, hierarchical environments. That playbook does not map onto a mid-size Kerala MSME without significant adaptation testing.
Metro-to-Kerala relocation failure. Candidates from Bangalore or Hyderabad decline Kerala offers not because of compensation — but because professional network continuity, peer community, and market access aren't addressed in the offer design.
Credentials over context. Hiring for titles and pedigree without testing whether the candidate can operate in a structurally different context. The hire looks right on paper; fails within 12 months in practice.
The Kerala Pattern

Company concludes it "can't find good people in Kerala." The actual failure: the search was scoped without testing for contextual adaptability — the variable that determines whether the hire works.

Delhi NCR · Noida · Delhi

Recruitment advisory
in Delhi NCR.
Noise is not depth.

Delhi NCR — spanning Gurugram, Noida, and Delhi — is India's largest hiring market outside Mumbai. It is also the most fragmented, the most relationship-driven, and the market most prone to title inflation and attribution complexity.

Recruitment advisory in Delhi NCR requires a different kind of filtering: distinguishing between candidates who have held authority and those who have actually exercised it.

Delhi NCR hiring failure points

Multiple agencies, one shortlist. Three contingency firms present the same five CVs. The illusion of market coverage; limited real depth.
PSU/government salary spillover. Compensation anchors from large hierarchical organisations create expectations that don't translate to high-accountability growth company environments.
Relationship-over-merit placements. Delhi's business culture rewards networks. Hiring decisions frequently reflect who knows whom — not who is most likely to deliver.

An Honest Assessment

Most recruitment partners optimise for placement speed.
Not decision quality.

This is a structural observation, not a criticism of individuals. Contingency models, thin retainers, and high-volume mandates create a business where the incentive to place fast is always stronger than the incentive to place right.

The contingency model misaligns incentives

Contingency firms earn nothing until a placement is made. The firm's revenue function and your outcome function are in direct conflict from day one. Expect a shortlist optimised for offer acceptance, not long-term fit.

No diagnostic work before searching

Most recruitment partners accept the brief as given. They don't challenge the JD, interrogate success criteria, or align stakeholders. When the brief is wrong — and it often is — the search produces candidates who fit a description that no longer matches the need.

Junior practitioners run senior mandates

The partner-level conversation happens at the pitch. The actual search — network engagement, candidate assessment, shortlist development — is then handled by a junior associate. The experience you paid for isn't doing the work.

Accountability ends at offer acceptance

Ask your current recruitment partner what happens when the hire exits at 9 months. If the answer is "we'll find someone else at a reduced fee," the incentive structure hasn't changed. The failure has simply been repriced.
